# ReplyPilot Creative Direction

## Strategic brief

ReplyPilot is not another AI writing toy. It is the private intelligence layer that helps a founder notice the right conversation, understand the missing angle, and act while the reply window is still open.

The visual system must communicate precision, timing, editorial judgment, and human control. It should make the product feel valuable before it makes it feel futuristic.

## Positioning territories explored

### 1. The Signal Desk

**Commercial promise:** See the conversations that deserve your attention before the window closes.

- Audience: solo founders, indie builders, and founder-led marketers.
- Occasion: a focused daily reply session.
- Business goal: reduce feed-scanning time and create more useful replies.
- Believable proof: ranked opportunities, age/timing signals, missing-angle analysis, three reply options, and a human send action.
- Visual implication: a compact editorial intelligence console; black and charcoal surfaces; fine timing traces; one orange live signal; product UI as evidence.
- Watch-out: do not turn this into a literal radar screen or military command center.

### 2. The Editorial Dispatch

**Commercial promise:** Enter each conversation with a point of view, not a template.

- Audience: opinionated founders whose voice is part of the product.
- Occasion: preparing a thoughtful reply or founder post.
- Business goal: improve reply quality and preserve voice.
- Believable proof: missing-angle summaries, reasons for each draft, voice examples, and edit history.
- Visual implication: independent magazine typography, precise annotations, cropped correspondence, warm paper details against dark chrome.
- Watch-out: avoid nostalgia, typewriter cliches, and a generic writer-tool aesthetic.

### 3. The Live Window

**Commercial promise:** Timing is not a score; it is a window that opens and closes.

- Audience: founders who already know that early replies compound visibility.
- Occasion: deciding what is still worth replying to right now.
- Business goal: increase the share of replies sent while a conversation is young.
- Believable proof: post age, velocity, conversation density, and transparent reasons.
- Visual implication: cropped time bands, cooling traces, thin arcs, countdown-like intervals, and a small orange interruption.
- Watch-out: avoid fake precision, speedometer graphics, and countdown anxiety.

### 4. Human at the Controls

**Commercial promise:** AI prepares the decision; the founder makes it.

- Audience: founders wary of spam, automation, and account risk.
- Occasion: reviewing three drafts before sending.
- Business goal: build trust and increase adoption of AI-assisted replies.
- Believable proof: three distinct options with reasons, editable copy, and explicit click-to-send behavior.
- Visual implication: restrained decision moments, a cursor or hand selecting one of three paths, visible authorship, no autonomous motion.
- Watch-out: avoid stock photos of people using laptops and generic hand-plus-interface compositions.

## Selected direction: The Signal Desk

The strongest route combines **The Signal Desk** with selected cues from **The Editorial Dispatch** and **The Live Window**.

### Creative idea

**A private editorial intelligence desk for live conversations.**

ReplyPilot should look like an instrument for judgment. The recurring visual story is a field of quiet conversation signals, with one opportunity becoming legible at exactly the right moment. The orange accent is not decoration; it marks the live window, active angle, or chosen action.

### Visual world

- Deep black outer chrome with charcoal work surfaces.
- Fine 1px rules, grids, traces, and annotated timing intervals.
- Warm off-white editorial typography against dark UI typography.
- Orange `#ff4400` used as a small live signal, never a background wash.
- Product UI fragments, reply rows, timestamps, handles, and decision states as the main proof.
- Occasional tactile details: matte anodized metal, uncoated black paper, subtle screen grain, registration marks, and cropped editorial annotations.
- Negative space and strong cropping rather than decorative illustration.

### Image language

Generated imagery should be abstract enough to support the interface but concrete enough to imply a working intelligence instrument. Use conversation traces, timing bands, intersecting routes, and one illuminated decision point. Avoid literal X logos, speech bubbles, brains, robots, rockets, neon cyberpunk, glowing AI orbs, holograms, mascots, and fake analytics.

### Typography

- Instrument Serif for campaign headlines and editorial statements.
- Inter for interface text and supporting copy.
- Geist Mono for timing, handles, labels, and operational annotations.
- Headlines may use serif italics for one strategic phrase, but never synthetic bold.

### Composition rules

- Let the product or signal field occupy 55-70% of the frame.
- Keep one clear focal point, usually the orange live-window signal.
- Use asymmetry and cropping to imply an active system extending beyond the frame.
- Use borders and surface steps for depth; no glass panels or heavy shadows.
- Preserve clean negative space for deterministic copy overlays.
- Never place more than one major message in an asset.

### Core campaign line

**Find the conversation before it cools.**

Supporting lines:

- Find the conversation. Take the angle. Reply before it cools.
- Your radar for better replies.
- AI-assisted replies. Human-sent, always.

### Low-budget asset system

One key visual can carry the first 90 days of launch activity:

1. Wide crop for the website hero and deck cover.
2. Square crop for X launch posts and founder updates.
3. Detail crops for feature announcements and quote cards.
4. Product screenshots over the same black/charcoal field for proof-led posts.
5. Plain typographic cards using the same rules when no new image is needed.

This keeps production economical: one visual world, one accent, one set of type roles, and product screenshots doing most of the selling.

## Asset brief

### A. Hero key visual

- Use: landing hero, deck cover, launch banner.
- Format: wide landscape.
- Content: dark editorial signal field with layered timing traces and one orange live opportunity.
- Copy: none inside the image; preserve negative space for layout.

### B. Launch social poster

- Use: X launch post, pinned post, founder announcement.
- Format: square.
- Content: a confident campaign poster built from the signal field and product-like timing fragments.
- Exact copy: `Find the conversation before it cools.`

### C. Product proof treatment

- Use: feature posts, sales deck, onboarding email.
- Format: wide landscape.
- Content: a dark product workbench showing ranked conversation rows, missing-angle analysis, and three reply choices without invented customer data or unsupported metrics.
- Exact UI labels may be added deterministically after the product screenshot is selected.

## Avoid list

- Literal radar screens or aircraft cockpit references.
- Cyberpunk neon, blue-purple gradients, glowing glass, and holograms.
- Cartoon AI, robot mascots, magic sparkles, and brain imagery.
- Viral-growth tropes, rockets, graphs shooting upward, and fake engagement scores.
- Busy collages, stock founder photography, and lifestyle scenes with no product proof.
- Orange used as a large background fill.
