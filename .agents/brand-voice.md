# Brand Voice Guide — ReplyPilot AI

> Built from brand-context.md, brand-strategy.md (section 08), brand-positioning.md, and design.md's tone note ("authoritative, literary, precise").

## 01 — Voice Overview

ReplyPilot AI sounds like a sharp builder who's already done the homework — someone talking to another builder who's short on time, not a marketing department addressing a persona. It never performs excitement it hasn't earned, never hedges a claim it can't back up, and never pads a sentence to sound more impressive. The voice is casual in rhythm but serious in substance: contractions and short sentences, but zero tolerance for hype, vagueness, or manufactured confidence.

**Voice Essence**: *ReplyPilot AI sounds like a sharp builder who's done the homework — direct, economical, and unimpressed by hype.*

## 02 — Tone Dimensions

**Formality** — Casual ●———→ Formal
Sits casual-but-not-sloppy. Contractions and short sentences throughout; never slang-heavy or performatively "relatable."
*Example: "Reply before the window closes." not "Yo, don't sleep on this window fr fr."*

**Energy** — Calm ●————→ Energetic
Calm and confident, not hype-charged. Confidence is communicated through specificity, not exclamation points.
*Example: "Three options. Pick one. Move on." not "🚀 Get AMAZING replies instantly!!!"*

**Humor** — Serious ————●→ Witty
Dry and occasional, never forced, never at the reader's expense. Closer to a knowing aside than a joke.
*Example: a wry one-liner about how everyone's inbox is already a firehose — never a meme format or forced pun.*

**Expertise** — Accessible ——●———→ Authoritative
Authoritative but never condescending — talks like a peer who's shipped things, not a professor lecturing.
*Example: "Writing was never the hard part. Timing is." — states the insight plainly, doesn't over-explain it.*

**Warmth** — Distant ——●———→ Intimate
Respectful, not cuddly. Warmth shows up as respect for the reader's time, not through friendliness performance.
*Example: "Your voice profile needs at least 10 tweets to train well." — helpful and plain, not "Hey there! Let's get your voice profile all set up! 😊"*

## 03 — Voice Qualities

**Direct**
Says the thing plainly, first, without three sentences of throat-clearing.
- **Do**: "Feed scanning is live. It surfaces high-opportunity conversations on a schedule you control."
- **Don't**: "We're thrilled to be rolling out an exciting new capability that we think you're going to love..."

**Confident, Not Boastful**
States what the product does well without reaching for inflated language — the claims are specific enough they don't need decoration.
- **Do**: "Every option comes with a reason it's worth sending."
- **Don't**: "Our revolutionary AI delivers world-class reply suggestions."

**Economical**
Every sentence earns its place. If a line can be cut without losing meaning, cut it.
- **Do**: "Three options. Not ten."
- **Don't**: "We give you a curated selection of three thoughtfully generated options, rather than overwhelming you with ten."

**Transparent**
Says what the product can't do yet as plainly as what it can — the same instinct that killed fake engagement scores in the product itself.
- **Do**: "We don't show engagement scores yet — we don't have the data to back them."
- **Don't**: "Our AI predicts engagement with industry-leading accuracy."

**Builder-to-Builder**
Talks like a peer who's shipped things, not a brand addressing a target demographic.
- **Do**: "You already know replies drive growth. Here's the part that's still manual."
- **Don't**: "Are you a busy professional looking to grow your social presence?"

**Unimpressed by Hype**
Actively avoids category buzzwords — the actual differentiation doesn't need decoration.
- **Do**: "Voice-matched writing is already solved. Finding the right moment isn't."
- **Don't**: "Harness the power of next-generation AI to unlock unprecedented growth."

## 04 — Vocabulary

**Words we use**: reply, window, timing, worth replying, discovery, conversation, ship, builder, signal, moment, precise, sharp, earn (as in "earn trust"), reason (never "score" without real data behind it), your voice, catch (the window), scan, surface (verb — "surfaces the conversations worth your time").

**Words we avoid**: revolutionary, game-changing, supercharge, unlock, seamless, effortless (implies laziness, not precision), 10x, next-level, cutting-edge, growth hacking, leverage (as jargon-verb), synergy, engagement (used vaguely, as a buzzword rather than the specific metric it is in-product).

**Brand-specific language**: "conversation," not "post," for what's being replied to. "Reply window," not "opportunity window" or other synonyms — one term, used consistently. "Voice profile" (already the product's own term — keep it exact). Address the reader directly as "you" in marketing copy; avoid "users" or "customers" outside of internal/product documentation — this audience identifies as builders, not as a demographic segment.

## 05 — Writing Style Rules

**Sentence length**: Short to medium, deliberately clipped for emphasis. A short sentence used as a full stop is a feature, not a fragment to fix: *"Three options. Not ten."*

**Punctuation**: One em-dash per paragraph, max — used for a single aside, never stacked. No ellipses — they read as trailing off or uncertain, which contradicts a confident voice. Exclamation marks: effectively banned; at most one per page, and only where genuinely earned.

**Capitalization**: Sentence case everywhere, including headlines — matches the editorial, literary framing already established by Instrument Serif in `design.md`. Shouty title-case marketing headlines would fight the visual system, not support it.

**Numbers**: Numerals for anything countable or precise (3 options, 2-hour window, 15 minutes) — numbers are part of the brand's credibility signal, so they should read as data, not prose. Spell out only at the very start of a sentence.

**Contractions**: Use them always — "don't," "it's," "you're." Avoiding them would push the voice toward corporate-formal, which contradicts the casual-but-serious tone.

**Active vs. passive**: Active voice, almost without exception. Passive voice reads as evasive, and this brand's whole ethos is being direct about what it does and doesn't do — including admitting fault (see Before/After #5 below).

**Humor**: Allowed, dry, rare. Never a joke at the reader's expense, never meme-format, never forced for engagement's sake.

## 06 — Channel Adaptations

| Channel | Tone Adjustment | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Website homepage | Slightly more crafted — pull quotes, serif headlines, room to breathe | "Built for the two-hour window." |
| Social media (X) | Maximally economical — the brand should practice its own "don't waste the reader's time" ethos here more than anywhere | "Everyone else helps you write. We help you find what's worth writing." |
| Product updates / email | Direct and specific about what changed, zero filler | "Feed scanning is live. Here's what it does." |
| Customer support / in-app help | Plain and helpful, no jargon, still no exclamation marks | "Your voice profile needs at least 10 tweets to train well." |
| Error messages / UI copy | Honest about what went wrong, no cutesy deflection | "Couldn't reach X — try again in a moment." |

## 07 — Before and After Examples

**Before (off-brand):**
> Unlock the power of AI to supercharge your X growth with revolutionary reply suggestions!

**After (on-brand):**
> Find the conversation worth replying to — before the window closes.

**Why it works:** Cuts every hollow adjective and leads with the actual mechanic (timing), which is the real differentiator, not "AI."

---

**Before (off-brand):**
> Our AI has a 92% engagement success rate!

**After (on-brand):**
> Every option comes with a reason it's worth sending — not a score we can't back up yet.

**Why it works:** Turns an honesty constraint already baked into the product into a voice strength, instead of hiding it.

---

**Before (off-brand):**
> We're so excited to announce our brand new Feed Scanner feature!!! 🎉

**After (on-brand):**
> Feed scanning is live. It surfaces high-opportunity conversations from your following feed on a schedule you control.

**Why it works:** Removes performed excitement and leads with what the feature does for a time-scarce reader.

---

**Before (off-brand):**
> Don't miss out on this amazing opportunity to 10x your engagement!

**After (on-brand):**
> The best reply window on a viral tweet is usually under two hours. Here's how not to miss it.

**Why it works:** Replaces vague hype-metric language with the specific, credible insight the whole product is built on.

---

**Before (off-brand):**
> We're sorry for any inconvenience this may have caused.

**After (on-brand):**
> That shouldn't have happened. Here's what we're doing about it.

**Why it works:** Direct accountability instead of hedge-y corporate apology — matches "confident, not boastful" and "transparent."

## 08 — Things We Never Say

- Hollow hype: "revolutionary," "game-changing," "10x," "supercharge," "unlock," "next-level," "cutting-edge"
- Fake precision: invented percentages or scores not backed by real data — this is a product rule as much as a copy rule
- Over-apologetic language: "We're so sorry for any inconvenience," "Oops!," excessive hedging
- More than one exclamation mark per page, ever
- Corporate deflection via passive voice to avoid ownership ("mistakes were made")
- False urgency unrelated to the real reply-window mechanic ("Only 3 spots left!")
- Cutesy error copy or emoji in error states ("Oopsie," "Something went wonky")
- Calling the audience "customers" or "users" in marketing-facing copy — they're builders, addressed directly as "you"
